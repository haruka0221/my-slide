PART II

Historical Background of CLS

The question of authorship attribution predates the computer by a very long time.

Attribution Anxiety — 1909

A Novelist Who Feared Computational Literary Study

"One may care infinitely little for the fate of one's work and yet be almost hypochondriacally anxious as to the form its publication shall take—if the publication is likely to occur posthumously. I became at once dreadfully afraid that some philologist of that Posterity for which one writes, might, in the course of his hyena occupations, disinter these poor bones and, attributing sentence one to writer A and sentence two to B, maul at least one of our memories. With the nature of those crimes one is only too well acquainted." — Ford Madox Ford, "Preface," in The Nature of a Crime (1924), xi. (co-authored preface with Joseph Conrad)
In 1909—77 years before Burrows's computational stylistics (1986)—Ford called sentence-by-sentence authorship attribution by philologists "hyena occupations."
▶ Anxiety about computational literary study is older than the computer itself. It was a question recognized even by literary practitioners outside academia.
History of CLS

A Genealogy of Computational Literary Studies

1851–1901 De Morgan & Mendenhall — prototypes of authorship attribution. Published in Science and Popular Science Monthly by physicists and mathematicians.
1986 Burrows, Computation into Criticism — function-word frequency analysis of Austen. The founding work of computational stylistics.
2000–2005 Moretti — proposes "distant reading." New Left Review (2000) → Graphs, Maps, Trees (Verso, 2005). Stanford Literary Lab founded 2010.
2012 Heuser & Le-Khac (SLL Pamphlet 4): "telling → showing" hypothesis in 2,958 British 19c novels. Same year Underwood & Sellers (Journal of Digital Humanities) rebut: lexical change is not novel-specific but reflects a long-term reversal across English as a whole — a paradigm case of CLS-internal correction.
2013–2018 Jockers, Macroanalysis (2013); Underwood, Distant Horizons (2019). Jockers & Kirilloff (2016): quantify gender differences in character depiction via pronoun + verb combinations. Underwood, Bamman & Lee (2018): long-term changes in gender representation in English fiction.
2021 Weidman & Pastor: analyze Woolf's Orlando with a binary gender classifier → style shifts at the gender-transformation scene. A case where a "wrong" framework opened a new reading.
2023 Walsh — Computational Reception Studies: a move from text analysis toward platform and reader-reception data.
Distant Reading

What Is Distant Reading?

"The trouble with close reading is…it necessarily depends on an extremely small canon… If you want to understand literature as a system, you need a different kind of approach." — Franco Moretti, Graphs, Maps, Trees (Verso, 2005)

Close Reading

  • Careful reading of the interior of a text
  • Nuance, context, authorial intention
  • A canon of tens to hundreds of works

Distant Reading

  • Quantitative analysis of thousands to tens of thousands of works
  • Patterns, trends, historical change
  • "The great unread"
Moretti's shift: from "carefully reading and interpreting texts" to "count them, graph them, put them on maps."
The Stanford Literary Lab (founded 2010) became the institutional home of this vision, accumulating empirical research using distant reading.
PART III

A Genealogy of DH Critique

Fish (2012) → Da (2019) → Spivak (2003 → DH application)
Three different directions of critique.

Critique I — Fish (2012)

Stanley Fish: A Critique of Computation without Interpretation

Fish's position: intentionalist hermeneutics

The goal of literary criticism is "to carefully read and decode the meaning that a text intends." That meaning comes into existence only through context, intention, and the interpretive community. Quantitative patterns are meaningless without this framework.
"The digital humanities…have little place for the investigations…in the business of asking and answering…what does it mean?" — Fish, "Mind Your P's and B's," NYT, Jan. 23, 2012

Fish's example: Milton's Paradise Lost

Computer analysis shows a high frequency of labial and plosive consonants (P, B) in the scenes depicting the fallen angels (Book I). But what does that pattern actually mean? Without the interpretive framework of Milton's theology, poetic tradition, and intention, the numbers remain meaningless. The title "Mind Your P's and B's" encapsulates this critique in one ironic phrase.
DH rebuttal (Ramsay 2011, et al.): "The algorithm does not replace interpretation—it guides us toward new readings. A pattern is not an answer; it is a starting point for posing questions." — Reading Machines (U of Illinois P, 2011)
Critique II — Da (2019)

Nan Z. Da: "The Methods of CLS Are Fundamentally Flawed"

"What is robust is obvious and what is not obvious is not robust." — Da, "The Computational Case against Computational Literary Studies," Critical Inquiry 45, no.3 (2019): 601–639

Methodological critique

  • The stopword dilemma: keeping function words yields noise; removing them erases differences
  • LDA instability: topics change dramatically with minor data alterations; reproducibility is problematic
  • Re-examination of existing studies: statistical validity questioned in work by Piper, Long & So, and Algee-Hewitt
  • Rejecting the "big data" myth: claiming ML is necessary for datasets that do not require it is an exaggeration
  • Mismatch of purpose: statistics "reduce meaning" while literary study "reads the complexity of meaning"
Published in CI 2019. The most systematic and empirical critique of CLS, it generated a major controversy that expanded into the CI online forum.
The Forum — CI Online 2019

CI Online Forum 2019: The Structure of the Debate

Multi-party debate on the Critical Inquiry official blog (March 2019–)

RespondentPositionKey argument
Algee-Hewittpro-DHDa judges all of CLS by the standards of CDA (confirmatory data analysis), but what matters in literary research is EDA (exploratory data analysis)—finding patterns in data without prior hypotheses. Statistics do not replace reading; they lead to new readings.
Underwoodpro-DHTopic models produce different results each run, but that is a premise of the method, not a flaw. Da misreads an exploratory tool as a hypothesis-testing instrument.
Long & Sopro-DHDa's critique removes one feature from a model that works through multiple combined features—an unfair move. Applying a haiku classifier to classical Chinese poetry also ignores historical context.

Bodedefinitional critiqueDa's definition of CLS is limited to ML-based word-pattern analysis, excluding at the outset important digital literary research in data construction, curation, and metadata analysis.
PART IV

Expanding the Definition of CLS and Connecting to Reception Studies

Before the Da debate, Liu (2012) asked "Where is cultural criticism in DH?"
and problematized the gap between DH practice and critical theory.
In the Da debate, Bode, Klein, and others took up that question, arguing for expanding CLS to include bibliography, circulation, data construction, and curation.
Walsh (2023) opened it further toward reader reception and platform data.

Expanding the Scope of CLS

Bode: Expand the Object of Study, Not Just the Method

Katherine Bode (2017)

"not new or integrated methods but a new scholarly object" — Bode, "The Equivalence of 'Close' and 'Distant' Reading," Modern Language Quarterly 78.1 (2017), pp. 77–106
Rather than a "new method" integrating close and distant reading, Bode argues for redefining the very "object of study" that computational literary research should address.

Key arguments

  • The methods of Moretti and Jockers tend to model the literary system abstractly and ahistorically.
  • The complexity of the "document record"—encompassing bibliography, publication, editions, and circulation—must be part of the object of study, not just the texts themselves.
  • The challenge for CLS is not only "which method to use" but "how to construct what we are studying."

Bode, Katherine. "The Equivalence of 'Close' and 'Distant' Reading; or, Toward a New Object for Data-Rich Literary History." Modern Language Quarterly, vol. 78, no. 1, 2017, pp. 77–106.

Expanding the Scope of CLS

Klein: Include Invisible Labor and Editorial Work

Lauren F. Klein (2019/2020)

"the multiple forms of labor—affective as much as intellectual" — Klein, "What the New Computational Rigor Should Be," Critical Inquiry online forum, 2019
Klein understands computational literary research not only as analytical output but as a practice encompassing data construction, editing, curation, and mentoring.

Key arguments

  • Datasets do not exist naturally; they are made through selection, repair, organization, and description.
  • Such work is intellectual labor that nonetheless tends not to be recognized as a research output.
  • Limiting CLS to vocabulary-pattern analysis renders invisible both the labor of making data and its politics.

Klein, Lauren F. "Dimensions of Scale: Invisible Labor, Editorial Work, and the Future of Quantitative Literary Studies." PMLA, vol. 135, no. 1, 2020, pp. 23–39.

New Directions

Walsh (2023): Computational Reception Studies

"an emerging subfield that explores questions about reading, reception, and the circulation of texts" — Walsh, "The Challenges and Possibilities of Social Media Data," in Debates in the Digital Humanities 2023, pp.275–294 (U of Minnesota P)

Traditional CLS

  • Computational analysis of literary texts themselves
  • Corpus → discovery of patterns and trends
  • Genre classification via style, vocabulary, and topic modeling
  • Examples: Moretti (distant reading), Jockers (macroanalysis), Underwood (long-term change in literary history)
Question: How are texts written?
→ Exploring structure and patterns "inside" works

Reception Studies

  • Goodreads review and rating data (the entanglement of "classic" concepts and Amazon)
  • Social media citation patterns: how James Baldwin's words were re-circulated in the context of the BLM movement
  • Public library, publishing, and platform data
Question: How have texts been read?
→ Making visible the entanglements of platform, economy, and power

PART VI

The Role of Primary Text Analysis

Not to replace critical intuition,
but to check its premises through frequency, distribution, comparison, and authorship attribution.

Example · Critical Definition

Two Kinds of Evidence within a Critical Definition

"As Jameson and other critics have observed, utopias are fundamentally typified by a break, marked by islands and trenches in both space and time, whereas I think dystopias tend to be characterized by a creeping, incremental horror, typical of the dreadful." — Robert T. Tally Jr., The Fiction of Dread: Dystopia, Monstrosity, and Apocalypse (Bloomsbury, 2024), p. 4
utopia
Defined by appeal to prior critics (Jameson et al.)
dystopia
Introduced via I think—the author's own critical judgment
Primary Text Strategy

Positioning Primary Text Analysis

Frequency
Confirm the occurrence of specific vocabulary and conceptual terms. A basic check on critical impressions.
Distribution
Examine skews across corpora, periods, and genres. Use topic modeling etc. to identify tendencies.
Comparison
Compare differences across genres, authors, and periods to relativize the premises of a reading.
Authorship attribution
Verify co-authorship, attribution, and stylistic differences to check the bibliographic facts that criticism takes for granted.
PART VII

Toward a Topography of Reception, Criticism, and Bibliography

Rethinking CLS beyond the vocabulary-pattern analysis of primary texts to encompass data construction, curation, reception, and circulation.

Building on this lineage, the present research takes as its starting point the actual evidentiary structure of English literary criticism—that roughly 70% of critical texts are devoted to the words of other critics—and constructs a topography describing multiple routes of literary evaluation.

Part VII — Integrated Approach

Mapping the Discursive Landscape

Literary research relies not only on primary texts but on criticism, citation, and institutional evaluation. This research measures not only the features of works themselves but where they become visible.
Scholarly attentionHow prominently does a work appear in research articles?
Cultural circulationHow widely does a work circulate through editions and library holdings?
Reader receptionHow visible is a work in the form of general-reader rating and review counts?

The goal is not to produce a single canonical ranking but to describe the overlaps and gaps across evaluation circuits.

Three independent evaluation circuits

Part VIII — Case Study

Case Study: The Endurance of Heart of Darkness

A canonical work with a troubled legacy

Chinua Achebe’s 1975 essay “An Image of Africa: Racism in Conrad’s Heart of Darkness” is often cited as a pivotal moment in literary criticism, marking a significant shift in the evaluation of Conrad’s work and its place in the canon.
Case Study

A Conradian Discomfort

Achebe’s intervention did not simply criticize Heart of Darkness; it also challenged the inherited Conrad canon and anticipated a shift toward culture, race, and colonialism. Andrew Purssell, “Making the Conrad Canon,” in The New Cambridge Companion to Joseph Conrad (2015)
As a Conrad scholar, I began from a disciplinary intuition: after Achebe, Heart of Darkness should be a troubled, overburdened, and perhaps declining object of study.
But the data produced a different question: why does Heart of Darkness remain so central across research, pedagogy, and reception datasets?

Interpretation

Reinstitutionalization through Critique

Heart of Darkness did not simply decline after Achebe.
It remained visible in research, central in pedagogy, and increasingly framed through the vocabularies of Africa, racism, imperialism, teaching, and contextual criticism.
This is not the survival of an untouched canon. It is a case of canonical reinstitutionalization through critique.

Working claim: the most contested canonical objects may be precisely the ones through which disciplinary change becomes institutionally stabilized.
