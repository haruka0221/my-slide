PART I

English Literature and Digital Humanities

A literary work is read as a text—but it always reaches us through the layers that surround it: criticism, citation, teaching, editions, reviews, reception. This talk asks how computational methods can address those layers, beginning with how DH itself appears in major literary-critical journals.

PART II

From Pattern to Literary Evidence

In English literary studies, DH often appears not as everyday method,
but as an object of critique. Yet the same debate also widens the question
of where literary evidence is found—and what counts as literary data.

PART IV

Heart of Darkness: Reproduced through Critique

Achebe's 1975 essay challenged Conrad's place in the canon—yet the work did not disappear. A critique, rather than removing a work, can remake how it is read, taught, and received.

Case Study

A Conradian Discomfort

Achebe's intervention did not simply criticize Heart of Darkness; it challenged the inherited Conrad canon and anticipated a shift toward culture, race, and colonialism. — Purssell, "Making the Conrad Canon," The New Cambridge Companion to Joseph Conrad (2015)

What happens after such a challenge?

Interpretation

Canonical Visibility through Critique

OBSERVATION
Heart of Darkness has not faded since 1975—it remains one of the most visible works across the topography.
DATA
JSTOR shifts toward race, empire, and teaching; Open Syllabus pairs the text with Achebe's Things Fall Apart.
READING
The work stays central not despite critique, but through the frameworks that critique made central.

Working claim: contested works can remain central because critique becomes part of what sustains their visibility.

Conclusion

Conclusion: Reading the Layers

A work's value is shaped not by the text alone,
but by the layers around it.

What we cite, teach, preserve, review, and overlook.

Digital methods connect those scattered traces—
not one ranking, but a profile of visibility.
