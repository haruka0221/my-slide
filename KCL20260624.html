PART I

English Literature and Digital Humanities

To what extent has DH become a part of the everyday practice of English literary criticism? This question is explored through a bibliometric analysis of 15 major journals, revealing the current state of DH's influence and presence in the field.

Starting Point

DH as a Methodological Commons

Willard McCarty & Harold Short
"Methodological Commons" (updated version provided by Short, 2020)
via Kiyonori Nagasaki, "Methodological Commons: デジタル人文学で昔から定番の話" , digitalnagasaki.hatenablog.com (2020/12/20)

“the field has become institutionalized and is no longer a novelty” — Drucker, “Normative Digital Humanities” p.7.
Research question:
If DH is now institutionally visible, how far has it entered the everyday practice of English literary criticism?

PART II

DH Critique as a Point of Contact

In English literary studies, DH often appears not as everyday method,
but as an object of critique. Yet these critiques also open a path
toward an expanded view of literary data.

DH in English Literary Studies

Pattern, Scale, and Interpretation

Computational literary studies entered English not only as a set of tools, but as a challenge to the ordinary scale and logic of literary criticism.

Scaling literary history

Moretti, Jockers, Underwood

“there are thirty thousand nineteenth-century British novels out there ... no one has read them, no one ever will” — Moretti, “Conjectures on World Literature” (2000)

Literary history becomes a problem of scale: corpora, patterns, trends, genre systems, and the “great unread.”

Questioning literary evidence

Fish, Hoover, Liu

“first you run the numbers, and then you see if they prompt an interpretive hypothesis” — Fish, “Mind Your P’s and B’s” (2012)

The critical question is not whether patterns exist, but when patterns become interpretation, evidence, or literary value.

This tension is not simply a rejection of DH. It is a debate over how computational patterns can become meaningful within literary criticism.

Critique — Da (2019)

Da: Turning the Objection into an Empirical Critique

"What is robust is obvious, and what is not obvious is not robust." — Da, "The Computational Case against Computational Literary Studies," Critical Inquiry 45.3 (2019): 601–639

Technical critique

  • Stopword choices can change what becomes visible.
  • LDA topic models may be unstable across runs and settings.
  • Existing CLS studies are re-examined for statistical validity and reproducibility.

Literary-critical critique

  • Computational results may be either obvious or statistically fragile.
  • Word-pattern analysis may not produce sufficient literary-critical payoff.
  • The essay raises a broader question about whether CLS can speak to literary interpretation.
Da’s essay became a flashpoint because it translated a familiar literary-critical suspicion into a direct critique of published CLS methods.
The Forum — CI Online 2019

After the Da Debate

Two Directions for Expanding Literary Data

After the debate, one possible response is not simply to defend existing methods, but to expand the kinds of evidence that CLS can study.

Bode: documentary records

"not new or integrated methods but a new scholarly object" — Bode, "The Equivalence of 'Close' and 'Distant' Reading" (2017)

Focus: editions, publication histories, metadata, bibliography, and the documentary record through which literary works are represented.

Walsh: reception data

"a rich archive of reading, literary reception, and textual circulation" — Walsh, "The Challenges and Possibilities of Social Media Data" (2023)

Focus: social media and platform-based traces of reading, including tweeted quotations, Goodreads ratings, BookTok videos, fanfiction, and online reviews.

Bode, Modern Language Quarterly 78.1 (2017); Walsh, Debates in the Digital Humanities 2023.

PART IV

Toward a Topography of Reception, Criticism, and Bibliography

Rethinking CLS beyond the vocabulary-pattern analysis of primary texts to encompass data construction, curation, reception, and circulation.

Building on this lineage, the present research takes as its starting point the actual evidentiary structure of English literary criticism—that roughly 70% of critical texts are devoted to the words of other critics—and constructs a topography describing multiple routes of literary evaluation.

Integrated Approach

Mapping the Limits of Each Standpoint

Literary research relies not only on primary texts but on criticism, citation, and institutional evaluation. This research asks not only where works become visible—but why they remain invisible elsewhere.
Scholarly attention What literary scholarship has decided is worth writing about
Cultural circulation What publishers and libraries have decided is worth preserving
Reader reception What one platform's user base has decided is worth rating
Pedagogy What instructors have decided is worth assigning

Where vectors converge: canonical status has been consolidated across circuits.
Where vectors diverge: the gaps are the finding—not noise.

Difference from JSTOR

JSTOR L&L: Covers article titles in literary studies only. A work title appearing in an article title is already a relatively strong signal.

OpenAlex: Access to title/abstract allows refinement, but work title alone easily picks up general words, personal names, place names, or usage in other fields.

Core approach:
✓ Work title + author surname co-occurring in title/abstract → strong candidate
✓ Topic/concept saved as supplementary information
✓ Only ambiguous candidates reviewed manually or via LLM

Risk flags & review method

Readily adoptable candidates:
✓ Work title + author surname clearly co-occur in title/abstract

Risk flags:
Short title: 1-word title or very short title
Anomalous value: extremely high hit count on title alone
Divergence: large gap with JSTOR / Goodreads etc.
Surname noise: James / Ford / London etc.

LLM use:
Pass article title, abstract, work title, and author name to the model; ask whether this article mentions the given work—for ambiguous candidates only.

PART V — Case Study

Case Study: The Endurance of Heart of Darkness

A canonical work with a troubled legacy

Chinua Achebe’s 1975 essay “An Image of Africa: Racism in Conrad’s Heart of Darkness” is often cited as a pivotal moment in literary criticism, marking a significant shift in the evaluation of Conrad’s work and its place in the canon.
Case Study

A Conradian Discomfort

Achebe’s intervention did not simply criticize Heart of Darkness; it also challenged the inherited Conrad canon and anticipated a shift toward culture, race, and colonialism. Andrew Purssell, “Making the Conrad Canon,” in The New Cambridge Companion to Joseph Conrad (2015)
As a Conrad scholar, I began from a disciplinary intuition: after Achebe, Heart of Darkness should be a troubled, overburdened, and perhaps declining object of study.
But the data produced a different question: why does Heart of Darkness remain so central across research, pedagogy, and reception datasets?

Interpretation

Reinstitutionalization through Critique

Heart of Darkness did not simply decline after Achebe.
It remained visible in research, central in pedagogy, and increasingly framed through the vocabularies of Africa, racism, imperialism, teaching, and contextual criticism.
This is not the survival of an untouched canon. It is a case of canonical reinstitutionalization through critique.

Working claim: the most contested canonical objects may be precisely the ones through which disciplinary change becomes institutionally stabilized.
